The physical layout of Union Street Center presents both opportunities and challenges that influence collaboration, communication, and overall departmental cohesion. While the proximity of professional, graduate, and student staff offices behind the Center Desk encourages accessibility and teamwork, the distribution of office spaces and open design features create barriers to privacy and sustained collaboration.

Impact of physical space on collaboration and communication at Union Street Center

Office Configuration and Accessibility

For example, the Residence Life Coordinator for Student Development (RLC-SD) has a private office adjacent to those of the graduate assistants, while the Assistant Coordinator for Center Management (ACCM) and the Residence Life Coordinator for Operations (RLC-O) occupy separate offices within the same suite. Although this arrangement allows for daily interaction, the open ceiling structure and limited soundproofing make confidential conversations difficult to conduct, often requiring the use of external meeting rooms such as the Eigenmann Hall Conference Room or the second-floor conference room in Cedar Hall.

Privacy Challenges

This separation of spaces can disrupt workflow and diminish opportunities for informal connection - an essential component of a cohesive residence life team.

Staff Hierarchy and Communication Barriers

Physical Divide Impact

Additionally, the physical divide between professional staff and student employees, such as Resident Assistants (RAs) and Desk Assistants (DAs), contributes to a subtle sense of hierarchy that may hinder approachability. While the Center Desk itself is designed to promote engagement, its high-traffic and public nature limit the depth of interactions possible during busy hours. As a result, meaningful mentorship and ongoing staff development conversations often occur elsewhere, fragmenting communication across roles and functions.

Efficiency and Workflow Challenges

Graduate Assistant Office Positioning

The current office configuration also affects efficiency. The graduate assistant office - located directly behind the Center Desk - serves as a transitional space between the operational and developmental sides of the department. This positioning provides visibility and accessibility but can be logistically challenging when attempting to balance administrative responsibilities with student support duties. As both professional and graduate staff utilize overlapping spaces for meetings, programming preparation, and student interactions, competition for limited private areas can strain productivity and scheduling.

Opportunities for Improvement

Enhanced Collaboration Potential

Overall, the physical environment at Union Street Center reflects a structure that values accessibility and openness but requires greater intentionality to promote cohesion, privacy, and collaboration. A more unified and strategically designed office arrangement could enhance workflow, facilitate clearer communication, and support the holistic mission of the residence life department.

Theoretical Foundation: As Strange and Banning (2015) note, the effectiveness of a learning environment depends not only on its physical design but also on how that design supports human interaction and organizational purpose.

Key Takeaways

The analysis reveals that while USC's physical layout promotes accessibility and daily interaction, it also creates challenges for privacy, sustained collaboration, and efficient workflow. The open design and distributed office spaces, while fostering transparency, can hinder confidential conversations and meaningful mentorship opportunities. Future considerations should focus on creating more intentional spaces that balance accessibility with privacy, and that better support the diverse communication needs of the residence life team.
